Verifiable Cities

Digital Public Infrastructure
for Municipal Innovation

Verifiable Cities is an applied research initiative exploring how open digital infrastructure can improve coordination, resource allocation, and trust in digitized operations and public service delivery. Over the past several months, we’ve worked with municipal governments, city innovation labs, and civic technology organizations to identify opportunities to strengthen state and civic capacity through transparent digital systems. These discussions, spanning North America, Europe, and Asia-Pacific, surfaced a set of common pain points and opportunities. The concepts that follow synthesize these insights into six areas of focus.

Pilot Concepts

The following six concepts represent the strongest patterns from our conversations with cities and municipal innovation initiatives. Each addresses a different challenge applicable across municipal processes and systems, from climate resilience to housing.

Concepts can be considered individually or combined: for example, Programmable Policy (Concept #1) and Privacy-Preserving Digital IDs (Concept #3) together enable an automated policy flow where deliberative input from verified residents is embedded directly into the system's logic. Combining these two concepts addresses a gap frequently described by cities and related actors where participatory processes run separately from the policies and programs they're meant to shape.

1 Programmable Policy Demo
Policy & Operations Data-Driven Systems IoT
The Problem

Every city has policies designed to activate under specific conditions, such as when air and water quality drops below a safe level or when temperatures reach dangerous thresholds. Though these policies have clear trigger conditions and well-defined responses, operationalising them still depends on manually checking data, recognising a threshold has been crossed, and initiating the correct response. The gap between condition and action is where capacity to respond quickly to real-time needs is most constrained.

The Approach

While this is sometimes a failure of policy design, the system also lacks transparent enforcement infrastructure that can make applicable policies self-executing. Programmable policy provides the missing layer by linking policy conditions directly to real-time data sources. When a threshold is met, the system triggers the designated response (e.g. issuing public alerts, releasing emergency funding, flagging for human review) and every threshold trigger and consequent action is publicly logged and auditable. Community input efforts can also be integrated for setting policy thresholds and trigger conditions.

Example

When air quality sensors and other IoT devices detect readings crossing a defined threshold, then vehicle restrictions are automatically activated in affected zones (policy trigger), public advisories are issued (transparency trigger), and pre-approved funding is released to emergency response providers (financial trigger). This concept also applies to water quality and treatment protocols, heat and temperature-based protections, disaster and emergency response funding release, and outcomes-based vendor contract payments.

What We Need From a City Partner
  • An existing policy with clearly defined trigger conditions and responses that is currently enforced manually.
  • Access to or availability of a real-time data source for the trigger condition(s).
  • A department or team with knowledge on climate programs, data management and sensor systems that can map the current policy or program workflow.
2 Outcomes-Based Vendor Contracts
Compliance Public Spending Vendor Management
The Problem

Cities are increasingly tying vendor payment to measurable outcomes and performance metrics to strengthen accountability for public spending. However, this approach introduces significant compliance burdens on city staff who must now collect evidence, reconcile milestones against documentation, prepare approval packages, chase incomplete or incorrect submissions, and route multiple payment deliveries over the course of a contract. This delays payments to vendors who have already delivered, discourages smaller providers from applying to performance-based contracts due to the uncertainty and delays in receiving payment, and requires extra staff time and compliance effort.

The Approach

Contract milestones and financial conditions are first encoded as measurable checks against vendor-submitted data. If these checks pass, they unlock milestone-based payment to the provider for delivery of services. When documentation is submitted, the system validates it against contract-defined criteria and either routes payment for authorisation or flags gaps for human review. The provider's outcome data is verified against contract-defined metrics automatically: a milestone met triggers payment authorisation, a milestone missed triggers review, and the full attestation chain is audit-ready.

Example

Denver spent $274M on homelessness services over four years, but found that some providers were achieving permanent housing outcomes for as few as 2–4% of clients exiting their programmes. The city has since moved away from flat-fee contracts toward performance-based models, paying providers for specific outcomes like housing retention and reduced jail days. With programmable compliance, every submission, verification, and payment action is time-stamped and logged — creating a transparent, publicly inspectable record that the city, the vendor themselves, and the public can independently verify and track over time.

What We Need From a City Partner
  • An existing performance-based vendor contract, or willingness to develop a trial contract with an existing or new vendor.
  • Access to or description of the current verification and payment authorisation workflow.
  • A procurement, finance, or contract management team willing to co-design the automated verification layer.

3 Privacy-Preserving Digital IDs
Cybersecurity Resident Services Digital Identity
The Problem

Every interaction between a resident and their city requires proving something such as residency, eligibility, enrolment status, age, address, qualifications, etc. Today, proving any one of these facts means over-sharing: handing over a full document that contains far more personal information than the interaction requires, and repeating the same process with every department, programme and service. The city must also spend time and develop processes to collect and store this data and perform the relevant checks, along with the legal burden and data protection obligations that come with it. The result is friction for residents, liability for the city, and a growing attack surface as AI-generated synthetic identities and deepfake documents become harder to detect and much more ubiquitous.

The Approach

A simple city-issued digital credential that verifies specific facts about a resident: "lives in this jurisdiction," "is over 18," "is enrolled in programme X/Y/Z", without revealing any information beyond what is needed for that specific interaction. The resident experience will remain familiar: sign up once through a standard city portal, verify identity through existing processes, and from that point forward, every city service can confirm what it needs to know without collecting data on what it doesn't need to know. The underlying cryptography (zero-knowledge proofs) enables selective disclosure — the ability to prove a fact without exposing the data behind it to make data minimisation enforced at the technical level rather than just a policy goal.

Example

If a resident applies for a district bus pass requiring proof of residency, then the system verifies "this person is a resident of this district" (verification) without the transit office ever seeing their full address (privacy preservation), and the verification result is logged instead of recording the personal data collected (compliance). This concept also applies to benefits eligibility across departments, age-gated service access, civic voting and participation verification, programme enrolment confirmation, and cross-jurisdictional service portability.

What We Need From a City Partner
  • A service or set of services where residents currently prove identity by submitting full documents.
  • Understanding of what specific facts each service actually needs to verify versus what it currently collects.
  • A department willing to pilot selective disclosure for a specific resident interaction.

4 Cross-Agency Data Sharing
Privacy-Preserving Verification Resident Access
The Problem

City departments hold critical information about residents pertaining to health, housing, income, benefits, education, etc, but this data is siloed across agencies with different systems and legal frameworks created to prevent the creation of centralized databases due to privacy concerns. Because of these precautions, data sharing agreements can take months or years to negotiate, often for a single data element, and residents need to re-prove identity and eligibility from scratch with every agency and programme. Many give up the process of accessing support and oftentimes the people who need the most help bear the heaviest documentation burden.

The Approach

Zero-knowledge cryptography allows one agency to verify a specific fact from another's records, such as whether someone's income is below a threshold, without either agency seeing the underlying data. A proof is generated that proves a certain fact, however the raw data never has to leave an organization's database. As a result, no data sharing agreements are required because no data is ever shared. Combined with a portable, resident-held credential (see Digital ID pilot concept), a person can verify eligibility once and carry that status across every agency they interact with.

Example

A single parent working two jobs qualifies for reduced-rate transit, subsidised childcare, and rental assistance. Today, they take time off work to visit three separate offices, fill out three separate applications, and provide the same proof of income documents to three separate intake workers, each of whom enters the information into a different system. The process takes weeks and requires multiple follow-up visits. With a portable verified credential, the parent proves income eligibility once at the first office. At the second and third, they present the credential and the system confirms eligibility instantly without the agency ever seeing their pay stubs, tax returns, or employer details. No data leaves any database. No data sharing agreement is needed. The parent accesses all three programmes in a single week instead of three months. This concept also applies to benefits navigation, refugee and newcomer coordination, cross-departmental case management, and multi-provider coordination for vulnerable populations.

What We Need From a City Partner
  • Two or more departments or providers that serve an overlapping set of residents but cannot easily share resident data.
  • A description of specific eligibility or verification checks that are currently repeated across providers.
  • A team willing to map the current intake workflow and co-design the credential-based verification layer.

5 Verifiable Permits, Licences & Certifications
Credentialing Cross-Jurisdiction Public Trust
The Problem

Cities issue thousands of permits, licences, and certifications every year in the form of paper documents or entries in local databases. These formats make it difficult to verify documents outside the issuing body, requiring manual contact to confirm authenticity.

The Approach

Every permit, licence, or certification issued by a city becomes a verifiable digital credential. Anyone — whether another department, jurisdiction, or member of the public—can verify authenticity instantly without contacting the issuing body. Revocation or expiry invalidates all copies simultaneously. Privacy-preserving cryptography ensures that no personal data can be extracted from the verification process, only the relevant fact ("this credential is valid") is confirmed.

Example

A family moves to a new district and needs to enrol their child in a new school. Today this means manually requesting and transferring transcripts and proof of enrolment from the previous school. With verifiable credentials, the parent carries digital records issued by the previous school that the new school can verify instantly and independently. This concept also applies to health inspection certificates, building and occupancy permits, professional and trade licences, and vendor qualification certifications.

What We Need From a City Partner
  • A permit, licence, or certification that the city currently issues in paper or digital form.
  • Understanding of how that credential is currently verified by third parties.
  • A department willing to pilot verifiable issuance for a specific credential type.

6 Public Works Investment
Public Works Municipal Finance Open Source City
The Problem

Investment and maintenance of public infrastructure like parks, libraries and urban green canopy is funded through municipal budgets that are increasingly constrained and often the first line item cut. When cities contract maintenance out, coverage is frequently incomplete with contractors not servicing every neighbourhood equally or leaving work unfinished. Residents who derive direct value from these assets have no mechanism to invest in their upkeep, and those who already informally maintain public spaces have no official framework to coordinate within or be compensated for that contribution.

The Approach

A democratized investment mechanism and maintenance scheme, structured as a sidecar alongside existing municipal financing avenues, that allows residents and local businesses to directly fund specific local infrastructure. Returns can be structured through well known avenues like carbon credits or usage-based revenue. Residents can take on upkeep work that the city cannot afford to contract or that contractors are not covering, with visible coordination of contributions, asset condition, and work needed.

Example

A city identifies a neighbourhood park where the maintenance contract covers structural repairs and routine seasonal upkeep. A community infrastructure bond for the park. A local café owner invests $500, a group of residents each put in $50, and a nearby business contributes $2,000. The bond funds a seasonal maintenance programme and generates returns for investors through carbon credits from the park's tree canopy. A retired resident who already mows the grass and trims hedges every week registers as a community maintainer, logs their work through the platform, and receives compensation from the fund which also received 3% of park revenue from the cafe and street vendor permit sales. The park's condition, fund balance, maintenance schedule, and contributor activity are all publicly visible and auditable.

What We Need From a City Partner
  • A public asset or set of assets where maintenance is underfunded or contractor coverage is incomplete.
  • Understanding of existing community-led maintenance activity, whether formal or informal.
  • Insight into existing revenue streams attached to or generated by the asset.
  • A Public Works team willing to co-design a bond-adjacent or bond mechanism.

Programmable Policy

Cities already have policies with clear triggers and defined responses. This approach connects them so that when a condition is met, the response happens automatically, transparently, and on the record.

Pilot Concept 1

The concept

Today

Every city has policies with clear trigger conditions and defined responses. The rules exist. The data often already exists too. But acting on them still depends on staff manually checking dashboards, sending emails, and routing approvals through chains of people. That gap between a condition being met and the response actually happening is where time and money are lost, and where residents wait longest.

With Programmable Policy

Policy conditions connect directly to real-time data sources so that when a threshold is met, the designated response fires automatically. Every trigger and every action is publicly logged and auditable. The policy doesn't change. The enforcement just becomes instant, transparent, and verifiable.

7A Real-World Example
Case Study

Catala — turning French law into executable code

Developed at Inria (France's national research institute for digital science), Catala is an open-source language that translates legislation line-by-line into verifiable, executable code. Their largest case study: formalizing France's housing benefits, covering ~13,000 lines of code for €18B/year in payments to 5 million households.

3Issues uncovered through digitization
📜 Legislative Text

Article D823-16, Code de la construction et de l'habitation:

"The monthly housing benefit is calculated based on the actual rent, capped at a ceiling determined by zone and household composition, minus a minimum tenant contribution calculated from household income and the number of dependents."

Exceptions in Articles D823-17 through D823-20 modify the calculation for shared custody, disability, and students under 25.

⌨️ Catala Implementation
# Each line maps to legislative text scope HousingBenefit: define rent_ceiling equals if zone = Zone1 and dependents = 0 then €319.87 if zone = Zone2 and dependents = 0 then €277.82 define benefit_amount equals min(actual_rent, rent_ceiling) - tenant_contribution(income, dependents) # Exception: shared custody exception define benefit_amount under condition shared_custody benefit_amount * 0.5
What the digitization process uncovered

Digitizing the policy required transparency, and transparency revealed a flaw and a fix

The policy

In 2017, France's highest court ruled that housing benefits must be split between both parents in shared custody arrangements, proportional to the time each parent has the child.

The problem

The existing system (built on legacy COBOL) couldn't compute benefits twice for the same household. Only parents who filed formal complaints got the correct amount. Everyone else received the wrong payment and had no way of knowing.

What digitization made possible
Translating the law into readable code made the gap between policy and implementation visible for the first time. It also turned policy into modular building blocks where related rules could be cleanly integrated with clear, data-backed interactions between them.

8What's New

Turning rules into code has already been proven — however, deploying programmable policy requires new trust infrastructure.

The Catala example shows that policy logic can be formalized and executed correctly. However, running automated decisions that move real public funds and affect real residents requires something more: proof that the system did exactly what it said it did, when it said it did it, and that no one changed the record after the fact.

This pilot adds a verifiability layer to programmable policy so that every trigger, action, and payment is recorded on a transparent, tamper-proof ledger.

This is the layer that makes automated systems for both policy execution and faster payment disbursement trustworthy enough for real deployment.
